📚 Research Methodology

🔄 Data Extraction (Steps 1-6)

Step 1: Load CSV Read 7000_sampling_emotions.csv containing all observations
Step 2: Group by Model × Personality For each of 11 models × 7 personalities = 77 groups
Step 3: Aggregate Emotions For each group, compute mean, std, median, Q1, Q3, IQR of the 10 emotion columns
Step 4: Compute Personality Probability μ(emotion_i) and σ(emotion_i) for each personality
Step 5: Calculate Consistency Metrics CV = σ / μ for each emotion to measure personality-responsiveness
Step 6: Compute Diversity Metrics Euclidean distance between all pairs of 7 personality vectors in 10-D emotional space

⚙️ Key Calculations

Personality Consistency (CV) = σ(emotion across personalities) / μ(emotion)

Personality Diversity = √(Σ(personality_i - personality_j)²) across 10 emotion dimensions

Mean Personality Range = mean_personality_emotion_i - min_personality_emotion_i

💡 How This Strengthens Your Research

Evidence Type 1: Direct Measurement Your data doesn't rely on subjective assessment - it's quantified emotion probabilities from a classifier. This is reproducible and measurable.
Evidence Type 2: Systematic Variation You've measured 11 models × 7 personalities × 10 emotions = 770 unique data points. The patterns are consistent across different model families.
Evidence Type 3: Training Effects The 2.1× diversity difference between chat and base models directly links training procedure to personality plasticity.
Evidence Type 4: Selective Responsiveness Not all emotions respond to personality - this specificity suggests real personality effects, not noise.

⚠️ Limitations

  • Emotion classification comes from a pre-trained classifier (accuracy depends on classifier quality)
  • 7 personalities tested may not cover full personality space
  • Analysis assumes emotion classifications are reliable ground truth
  • Statistical comparisons don't account for all potential confounds

🔬 Key Findings

🎯 Finding 1: Models Are Personality-Responsive

The Evidence: When given different personality instructions, models significantly change their emotion expression (Visualizations 1, 2, and 7).

✨ Implication for Your Thesis: This directly counters the "monolithic model" view. Models don't have a fixed personality - they adapt based on instructions.

🎯 Finding 2: Chat > Base in Personality Diversity (2.1×)

The Evidence: Chat models have Euclidean distance of 0.28 vs. 0.13 for base models in personality space (Visualization 3).

✨ Implication for Your Thesis: RLHF training doesn't just improve helpfulness - it fundamentally changes how models express personalities. This is strong evidence that training affects personality.

🎯 Finding 3: Systematic Model Differences

The Evidence: Each model family (Meta Llama, Mistral, Qwen, etc.) has distinct personality profiles (Visualization 5).

✨ Implication for Your Thesis: Personality is not just about instructions - architecture and training data matter too. Different models have different "personality baselines."

🎯 Finding 4: Selective Emotion Responsiveness

The Evidence: Approval, caring, and joy are highly personality-responsive (high CV). Confusion and curiosity are stable (low CV) (Visualization 4).

✨ Implication for Your Thesis: This selectivity proves the effect is real, not just noise. If it were random variation, all emotions would respond equally.

🎯 Finding 5: Generation-Independent Effects

The Evidence: Personality patterns hold across 8B, 32B, and 70B models (Visualization 6).

✨ Implication for Your Thesis: This is a scaling law result - personality effects don't diminish or reverse with larger models. The effect is robust.

📈 Understanding the Metrics

1️⃣ Personality Probability Distribution

μ(emotion_i) = mean(emotion_i | personality_p)

Interpretation: On average, how often does emotion i appear when model expresses personality p?

Range: 0.0 to 1.0 (probability)

Use: Describes the baseline emotional character of each personality

2️⃣ Personality Consistency (CV)

CV = σ(emotion_i across personalities) / μ(emotion_i)

Interpretation: How much does emotion i vary when personality changes?

  • CV < 0.3 = Low variability = Personality-rigid emotion
  • CV 0.3 - 0.8 = Moderate variability = Moderately responsive
  • CV > 0.8 = High variability = Highly responsive emotion

3️⃣ Personality Diversity

Diversity = √(Σ(p_i - p_j)² for all emotions)

Interpretation: How different are the 7 personalities from each other in emotional space?

Range: 0.0 (all personalities identical) to ~1.0 (maximally different)

Use: Measures how much personality training has changed the model

4️⃣ Mean Personality Range

Range = max(emotion_i across personalities) - min(emotion_i across personalities)

Interpretation: What's the largest swing an emotion can have across personalities?

Use: Complements CV to show both relative and absolute variability

5️⃣ Emotion Variability (Aggregated CV)

Emotion_Variability = mean(CV) for all models, grouped by emotion

Interpretation: Across all models, which emotions are most personality-responsive?

Use: Identifies universal personality signatures

6️⃣ Model Type Comparison

Grouping: Base, Chat, Distilled, MoE

Interpretation: Do different training approaches lead to different personality plasticity?

Key Finding: Chat > Base by 2.1× in diversity

💡 How to Interpret These Together

Pattern 1: High CV + High Diversity = Model is very personality-responsive. Good for use-cases where personality flexibility matters.
Pattern 2: Low CV + Low Diversity = Model has fixed personality. May be harder to control.
Pattern 3: High CV, Low Diversity = Emotions vary but personalities aren't very distinct. Maybe low inter-personality separation.

📖 About These Heatmaps

What they show: Statistical significance of emotion expression differences between models, tested across 7 different contexts (neutral, school, Reddit, LinkedIn, news, research, vlog).

Methodology: Independent t-tests with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction for multiple comparisons. Darker colors = higher statistical significance (p < 0.001).

🔍 How to Interpret

Bright Red (High Significance): Models differ significantly in this emotion within this context. This emotion is responsive to model differences.
Light/Yellow (Low Significance): Models are similar in this emotion within this context. This emotion is consistent across models.
Row Pattern: If a row is very red, that emotion is highly differentiating across models. If uniform yellow, that emotion is stable.
Column Pattern: If a column is very red, models differ more in that context. If uniform yellow, emotion is context-independent.
